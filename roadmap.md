### The Three Layers of Execution

Every day operates on three levels simultaneously:

**Layer 1 — The Tick (Did I show up?)**
Your daily checklist handles this. Copy the Weekly Rhythm Template to your
notes app or notebook. Tick items off. A tick means you did not skip.
This is the minimum.

**Layer 2 — The Quality (How present was I?)**
After each session, rate it 1–3:
- 1 = went through the motions, body was there, mind was not
- 2 = mostly engaged, some drift
- 3 = fully present, effortful, real work done

A day of 1s still counts as showing up. But track the numbers — if you are
consistently scoring 1s, something is wrong and your journal will tell you what.
Aim to increase your average over time, not to be perfect daily.

**Layer 3 — The Review (Am I actually changing?)**
This is where the roadmap becomes a living document. Your weekly and monthly
reviews are where growth gets processed and the document gets updated.
Most people skip this layer and wonder why they are not improving.

### How to Handle Slips — The Protocol

This section matters more than any other in this document.

When you slip on the porn/dopamine habit — and you likely will at some point —
the execution is:

1. Write it in your journal: what time, what triggered it, what you felt before
2. Add 1 to your urge/slip count for the week
3. Continue the next scheduled daily item as if nothing catastrophic happened
4. Do NOT declare the day ruined
5. Do NOT start a shame spiral
6. Do NOT restart your "streak" counter and treat it as day zero

A slip followed by continuing is infinitely better than a slip followed by
a shame spiral that wipes out a week. The recovery from the slip IS the training.

The same protocol applies to any missed day, any failed focus session,
any emotional crash. Log it. Continue. That is all.

### 1.1 Dopamine Reset Protocol

**Why this is first:** Your dopamine system is dysregulated from ~13 years of
hyperstimulation. This is causing your focus problems, dissociation, emotional
crashes, and boredom with real life. Nothing else will work properly until this
is addressed.

**What is happening in your brain:**
- Repeated intense stimulation has downregulated your dopamine receptors
- Ordinary tasks (thinking, reading, learning) no longer trigger enough dopamine
  to feel rewarding
- Your brain has been trained to demand instant, effortless stimulation
- This is not a moral failure — it is a measurable neurological condition

**The Strategy — Gradual Reduction with Friction:**

Week 1–2:
- Move all pornographic material to your old phone immediately (you already planned this ✓)
- Do NOT have the old phone in your bedroom or within arm's reach
- Goal: reduce to once per day maximum
- Every time the urge hits, impose a 10-minute delay before acting on it
- Do not white-knuckle it — use the delay to go for a walk or drink water

Week 3–4:
- Reduce to every other day
- Begin replacing the trigger (identify what precedes the urge — boredom? anxiety?
  procrastination? late night alone?)
- When the trigger hits, deploy a replacement: pushups, cold water on face, go outside

Important rules:
- **No shame spiraling when you slip.** A slip is data, not failure. Log what triggered it and move on.
- Quitting cold turkey after 13 years often backfires. Gradual reduction is more
  neurologically sound.
- The goal in Phase 1 is reduction and awareness, not perfection.

### 1.2 The 10-Second Pause (starts immediately)

Every time you feel the urge to switch tabs, open Twitter, open YouTube, or
pick up your phone during a task — **pause for 10 seconds first.**

Count slowly. Then you may go.

This sounds trivial. It is not. It is directly retraining your impulse control
circuitry. Do this religiously.

Extend by 5 seconds every week. By week 8, you'll be pausing 45 seconds.
By then, you'll often find the urge has passed.

---

### 1.3 Start the Self-Observation Journal

Get a notebook or use your phone's notes app. Every day, log:

```
DATE:
Mood when I woke up (1–10):
What triggered me today (distraction / crash / urge):
What I did instead (what was the avoidance behaviour?):
What I was feeling RIGHT BEFORE the trigger:
One thing I noticed about my own patterns:
```

Do this for all of Phase 1. You will see your patterns within 2 weeks.
Most people have 3–5 core triggers causing 90% of their problems.

### 1.4 The Non-Negotiable Physical Basics

These are not optional. Brain chemistry requires these:

- **Sleep:** 7–8 hours, consistent bedtime. No screens 30 minutes before sleep.
  Sleep is when your brain consolidates learning and resets dopamine receptors.
  Skipping this cancels everything else.

- **Daily walk:** Minimum 20 minutes outside. This is the single most
  evidence-backed free intervention for dopamine regulation, focus, and
  reducing dissociation. Do it even when you don't feel like it.
  Especially when you don't feel like it.

- **Water:** Drink enough. Dehydration directly impairs cognition and worsens
  anxiety. Aim for 2–3 litres daily.

- **Food:** Don't skip meals. Blood sugar crashes worsen emotional dysregulation
  and make everything harder.

### 2.1 Emotional Dysregulation — The Core Problem

Your current pattern:
**Obstacle encountered → immediate freak out → depression → quit**

This pattern is why no skill fully develops. L Lawliet stays *inside* the problem

This is not weakness. It is a trained nervous system response, likely developed

**The Free CBT Book:**

Work through it at one chapter per day. This book has actual clinical trial

- Breaking the thought → emotion → behaviour chain
- Rebuilding response to failure and obstacles

**Cognitive Distortions to Check Yourself For:**

### 2.2 DBT Skills for Distress Tolerance

DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy) was designed precisely for emotional
dysregulation. You can access the tools for free.

Search: **"DBT self-help worksheets free PDF"**

Key skills to focus on first:

**TIPP** (for when you're already in an emotional crash):
- Temperature: cold water on your face/wrists — activates the dive reflex,
  rapidly lowers heart rate and emotional intensity
- Intense exercise: 5 minutes of jumping jacks or pushups burns off the
  adrenaline
- Paced breathing: breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 8
- Progressive relaxation: tense and release each muscle group

**Opposite Action:**
When emotion tells you to withdraw/quit/hide — do the opposite deliberately.
When you hit an obstacle and feel like stopping — stay for 5 more minutes.
Just 5. Build from there.

**Free YouTube resource:** Search **"Crappy Childhood Fairy"** on YouTube.
Sounds odd. Genuinely useful for people who developed coping patterns young.

---

### 2.3 Dissociation — Direct Approach

Dissociation (zoning out, feeling unreal, going offline mentally) is your
brain's protective response to chronic stress or understimulation. It worsens
with heavy screen use and improves with:

- Physical grounding: the daily walk from Phase 1 is already helping this
- The 5-4-3-2-1 technique when dissociation hits: name 5 things you can see,
  4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste
- Reducing screen overstimulation (the dopamine work in Phase 1 addresses this)
- Consistent sleep schedule (your nervous system needs predictability)

---

### 2.4 Begin Rebuilding Focus Capacity

**Start absurdly small.** 5 minutes of focused single-task work.
Set a timer. Close everything else. Do one thing.

Then 10 minutes. Then 15. Build over weeks, not days.

This is called **progressive overload for attention** — the same principle
as building muscle. Your focus is currently weak from years of
fragmented, high-stimulation use. It will rebuild if you train it consistently.

Track your sessions in your journal.

**Free resource:** Huberman Lab on YouTube/Spotify.
Search: "Huberman focus protocol" and "Huberman dopamine."
These are long but dense with practical neuroscience you can apply immediately.

### 3.1 Logic and Analytical Thinking

**Books (find free PDFs online):**

- *The Art of Thinking Clearly* — Rolf Dobelli (cognitive biases)
- *How to Think* — Alan Jacobs

**Free practice:**
- Logic puzzles daily (search "logic grid puzzles free online")

- Lateral thinking puzzles — search "lateral thinking puzzles with answers"
- Argument mapping: take any claim and map out its premises and conclusions

**Daily habit:** One logic puzzle per day, minimum.

---

### 3.2 Probability and Bayesian Thinking

L constantly updates his probability estimates as new information arrives.
This is called Bayesian reasoning.

- Start with: search "Bayes theorem explained simply" on YouTube
- Practice by making predictions daily: "I think X is Y% likely because..."
  Then check if you were right. Track your calibration over time.

---

### 3.3 Observation and Deduction Practice

- **The daily observation drill:** Enter any room, spend 2 minutes, leave,
  then write down everything you noticed. Do this every day. Build up detail.
- When in public, form hypotheses about strangers based on observable details
  (occupation, mood, habits). You'll never know if you're right — that's not
  the point. The point is building the habit of reading environment and people.
- Study **microexpressions:** Search "Paul Ekman microexpressions free" —
  there are free training resources from his work online.

---

### 3.4 Memory Training

- Learn the **memory palace technique** (Method of Loci):
  Search "memory palace tutorial free" on YouTube
- **Spaced repetition:** Use Anki (free app) for anything you want to memorize
- Practice recalling what you read the day before before opening new material

---

### 3.5 Metacognition (Thinking About Your Thinking)

This is L's most distinctive skill. He watches himself think.

**Daily practice:**
After any period of focused work, ask:
- What assumptions was I making?
- Where did my reasoning go wrong?
- What would change my mind about this?
- What am I avoiding thinking about?

Write the answers. This single habit builds metacognition faster than anything else.

---

### 3.6 Broad Knowledge Base Building

L draws connections across domains. One topic per month, self-directed:

Suggested sequence:
1. Month 1: Psychology and human behaviour
2. Month 2: History (pick one era or civilization)
3. Month 3: Philosophy (start with *Sophie's World* — accessible and free PDF available)
4. Month 4: Criminology and forensic psychology
5. Month 5: Economics and game theory
6. Month 6: Biology and neuroscience basics

Rule: Read primary and secondary sources, not just summaries.
Summaries give you the conclusion. Sources give you the reasoning.

### 3.7 Social/Strategic Intelligence (Low-Pressure Start)

You don't need to be social. You need to be functional.

- Start with online text communities where you have shared interests.
  Practice articulating your ideas in writing. Defend positions. Engage.
- Study **negotiation psychology**: search "Never Split the Difference summary"
  (Chris Voss) — later read the full book
- Study **influence and persuasion** principles (Cialdini's *Influence*,
  free summary available online)
- In-person social contact can come later. Don't force it before you're ready.

## PHASE 4: L LAWLIET TRAINING
### *Month 9+ — The Full Protocol*

By this point the groundwork is laid. Now you can train all the intelligence
types you originally listed:

| Intelligence Type | Primary Method |
|---|---|
| Fluid + Analytical | Logic puzzles, chess, mathematics, novel problem-solving |
| Emotional Intelligence | DBT skills, observation practice, journaling |
| Executive Function | Focus sprints, planning systems, habit tracking |
| Metacognition | Daily self-interrogation practice (3.5 above) |
| Learning Intelligence | Spaced repetition, deliberate practice, Feynman technique |
| Practical Intelligence | Apply knowledge to real problems constantly |
| Social & Strategic | Case studies, negotiation study, real interactions |

**The Feynman Technique** (for Learning Intelligence):
1. Study a concept
2. Explain it as if teaching a child
3. Identify where your explanation breaks down
4. Go back and fill those gaps
5. Repeat until you can explain it simply

This is how you truly learn anything.

---

### 4.1 The L Lifestyle Elements (Evidence-Based)

- **Sit however you think best.** Posture that opens your chest tends to
  support confident thinking. Experiment.
- **Protect deep work time.** L works in isolation. Create a daily window
  where nothing interrupts your thinking.
- **Stay in the problem.** When you hit a wall, your new rule is: stay for
  5 more minutes before stepping away. Always.
- **Keep a case file.** On any problem you're working on, keep a running
  document of observations, hypotheses, and contradictions.
- **Question your own conclusions first.** Before defending an idea, attack it.
  If it survives your attack, it's worth defending.
